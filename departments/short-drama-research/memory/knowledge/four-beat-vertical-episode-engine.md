A 60–90 second vertical short-drama episode converges across overseas craft sources on the same four-beat shape. Use this as a checkable structure when reviewing or briefing a single episode:
1. **Hook / explosion point (0–15s, often 0–5s):** Open on the highest-tension frame the episode owns. Treat the first three seconds as a "freeze-frame trailer" — if a stranger needs context, the open is failing. Logos, intros, and wide establishing shots correlate with a 30–50% second-1-to-second-3 cliff drop and choke algorithmic reach. Burned-in captions ≥48–60pt are mandatory; ~80–85% of viewers start sound-off.
2. **Friction (15–60s):** Visible, filmable conflict — public, witnessed, prop-anchored. Subtext belongs in long-form, not here.
3. **Spike (60–90s, sometimes 45s):** A re-pricing beat — new evidence, POV flip, status shift. Mute test: if muting kills the spike, it was volume, not a spike.
4. **Button / cliffhanger (last 5–10s):** Cut on the question, not the answer. Cut 1–2 seconds earlier than feels safe. End on an object, a glance, or a freeze. The cliffhanger should hand the viewer a concrete prediction to type into comments.
Mid-clip discipline that keeps the engine running: micro-hooks every 20–50s, stimulus changes (cut, camera move, overlay swap, zoom, color shift) every ~2–4s. Below ~2.5 cuts/sec mid-clip, retention dips. Every act of harm should change a *tactical option* the protagonist has (location lost, contact burned, leverage given up), not just emotional weight — otherwise the show loops with cosmetic variation.
